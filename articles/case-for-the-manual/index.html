L&D: The Case for the Manual
(Yes, Even in 2026)

I was in for an MRI recently, sitting in my wheelchair in the hallway, when I noticed the MRI technicians huddled over a battered three-ring binder. Apparently, they were having difficulty finding the diagram necessary to perform my procedure. The confusion was obvious, and I offered to help however I could from where I sat. They handed me the binder, and inside were screen captures and hand-drawn diagrams of terminals and plugs, some laid out in tables, some full page, almost all of them covered in handwritten notes correcting or updating what the printed page said. A few pages had version control dates going back to 2015.

When I asked why so much of the binder was filled with handwritten notes, nobody had a good answer. But one thing I heard has stuck with me since: the only formal training the techs said they received was a short training video. After that, they were on their own, quietly stitching together the reference binder that should have existed from day one, in an environment where the stakes for getting a terminal or connection wrong are not small.

I sat there thinking about what I would have built instead, and I made myself a promise: I was going to write about why manuals (and job aids more broadly) still matter, even when video is right there and easy to produce.

The pendulum has swung toward video, hard

I don't think this is imagined. Video is faster to produce than it used to be, screen recording tools are everywhere, and stakeholders love how polished a talking head or software demo looks compared to a page of bullet points (also not always optimal). There's real research behind some of that appeal. Software demonstration walkthroughs, the kind you see from Adobe, Microsoft, and similar vendors, are considered the standard format for teaching people how to use an application, largely because they let a learner watch a task performed rather than read about it in the abstract.

But "video is engaging" and "video is sufficient" are two very different claims, and L&D has a habit of quietly treating the first as proof of the second.

What actually happens when video is the only thing offered

The Nielsen Norman Group ran a study specifically on this question, watching people try to learn tasks from instructional video versus written steps. What they found lines up almost exactly with what I saw in that MRI hallway: people wanted to read the instructions first and only turned to the video when they got stuck on a specific step. When a company made videos the only way to access a piece of information, like a product support page where a repair or maintenance step existed solely as a video, people reacted with visible frustration rather than gratitude for the format.

That's the binder, in a nutshell. The techs weren't rejecting their training video. They were reaching for exactly the kind of reference material Nielsen Norman's participants asked for, and building it themselves because nobody had.

There's clinical evidence pointing in the same direction. A randomized trial in the National Journal of Medicine examined whether a stand-alone instructional video could teach practical clinical skills to medical students. The researchers found the picture "controversial," in the researchers' own words, with mixed results across studies on whether video alone actually builds usable, retained skill. That's because video is genuinely good at building a mental model of how something works. It's a much shakier bet as the only tool to learn the skill you need to execute correctly under pressure months later, without someone rewinding a video clip for you.

Job aids exist because memorization was never the plan

There's a name for what that binder was trying to be: a job aid, sometimes called a performance support tool. The whole premise of a job aid is that you don't expect a learner to memorize every step. Instead, you give them a reference they can consult at the moment, which is precisely why successful instructional design programs include job aids as a part of training, not a replacement for it, which is especially valuable when a task is complex, high stakes, or infrequent enough that memory alone is unreliable. Check out this article from the University of San Diego for more information and tips on how to build effective job aids.

Bob Mosher and Conrad Gottfredson's framework, the Five Moments of Need argues that formal training covers the moments when someone is learning something brand new or building on existing knowledge; however, three other moments are handled far better by accessible, in-the-moment support than by another course or another video:

  • applying what was learned
  • solving a problem when something goes wrong, and
  • adapting to a change

A short onboarding video covers "learning new" reasonably well. It does almost nothing for the tech six months later, trying to remember which of four visually similar connectors go where, under time pressure, with a patient waiting. Check out more on working with the Five Moments of Need and how to retrain your focus to a learner's needs in this great article from the eLearning Coach.

Why the binder, specifically, was a documentation failure

None of this is an argument against video. It's an argument against treating video as a complete substitute for reference documentation, particularly in environments where:

  • The task is infrequent enough that people won't retain every step from a single viewing
  • Getting it wrong has real consequences (such as clinical, safety, compliance)
  • People need to look something up mid-task without stopping to find and scrub through a video
  • The information changes often enough that someone needs an easy way to update it

A well-built job aid, whether it's a quick reference card, a laminated one-pager, or, in this case, even a properly maintained binder with clear version control, solves all four of those problems in a way a video simply isn't built to. And when the aid doesn't exist, people don't stop needing it. They just build a worse version of it themselves, by hand, with a Sharpie, one outdated page at a time.

What I'd have done differently

If I were building that program, I'd have kept the video for what video does well: showing the workflow once, end-to-end, so a new tech has a mental model of the whole process. Then I'd pair it with a really great job aid: clean graphics of each terminal type, laid out for fast scanning, version controlled properly so nobody's cross-referencing a page from 2015, and reviewed on a schedule for accuracy instead of relying on whoever happened to notice something was wrong to update the material.

To be clear: this is not a nostalgia argument for paper over pixels. It's a performance support argument: match the format to the moment of need, not to whichever format is trendiest to produce this year. Sometimes that's a video. Sometimes it's a page you can flip to in four seconds with gloves on. Good instructional design has room for both, and honestly, needs both.
