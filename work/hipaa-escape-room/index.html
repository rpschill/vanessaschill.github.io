UNDER CONSTRUCTION: HIPAA Compliance Interactive Escape Room

An in-progress interactive module that reframes annual HIPAA training as a timed escape room — learners work through a series of privacy and security puzzles, each one built around a decision they'd actually face on the floor.

In development Gamification Scenario-Based HIPAA

Status

This case study is still being built

The module is in active development. The full write-up — design rationale, puzzle mechanics, assessment approach, and a playable demo — will land here once the build is complete.

In the meantime, here's where the project stands and what it's trying to solve.

Context

Compliance training people click through

Annual HIPAA training is near-universal in healthcare and near-universally tuned out. The format is usually a slide deck followed by a knowledge check, which is enough to satisfy the audit requirement but not enough to change what someone does when a chart is left open on a shared workstation or a family member asks a question in a hallway.

The gap isn't awareness — most staff can define PHI. It's recognition under pressure: spotting a violation in the moment, when it's inconvenient and the pressure is to keep moving.

Design approach

Puzzles as decision practice

Each room in the experience holds a realistic clinical environment — a nurses' station, a break room, a patient room — seeded with privacy and security issues the learner has to find and correct to unlock the next door. The clock creates the time pressure that makes the practice feel like the job rather than a quiz.

Wrong answers cost time instead of ending the attempt, so the module keeps learners exploring rather than guessing at the "training-correct" response. Every lock and clue maps back to a specific HIPAA Privacy or Security Rule provision, so the game layer never drifts from the compliance content it's built to teach.

What's next

Build and pilot

Remaining work: finishing the interactions for the later rooms, wiring up scoring and completion tracking for LMS reporting, and running a small pilot to check whether the timing and difficulty hold up with a real audience.
